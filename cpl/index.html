How the Rating works

Rating estimates the net points per game you add versus a league-average player, after adjusting for who you played with and against. Think of it as a swap test: keep your partner and opponents fixed, replace you with a generic average player, and the rating is how much the scoreline changes. 0 is average, + is above average, − is below. Since games go to 11/15/21, a swing of ±3 per game is large.

It isn't raw point differential. Pile up a big margin alongside a strong partner against weak opponents and the model credits the partner and discounts the weak opposition — so your number lands lower than the scoreline. Win a tight one with a weak partner against strong opponents and it can rate higher than the score suggests.

Opp Str and Partner Str make that context visible: each is the average rating of the opponents you faced / partners you played with, on the same points/game scale (above 0 = stronger than league average). They explain why a rating can diverge from a win-loss record — e.g. an undefeated player with high Partner Str and low Opp Str had favorable conditions. Click any player for a plain-English summary.

The whole league is solved at once, and recomputed from scratch every update. Everyone's rating depends on everyone else's, so results are retroactive: lose to an unknown who later turns out strong, and that loss is automatically re-credited as "a loss to a strong player" — no new game required. Beat someone who later proves weak, and the win quietly loses value.

The Conf column is how much of each rating is driven by real game evidence rather than the average-player starting assumption (technically, the share of variance the data has resolved). It rises as a player logs more games against well-connected opponents. Early-season numbers sit in the middle and move a lot — read a +3.0 at 68% as far more settled than the same rating at 45%.
